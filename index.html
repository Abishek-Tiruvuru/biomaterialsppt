Iron-Carbon Phase Diagram

A Simple Visual Guide

What is This Diagram?

The Iron-Carbon Phase Diagram is like a map that shows what happens to iron when you:

  • Add different amounts of carbon (0% to 6.67%)
  • Change the temperature (from room temp to 1600°C)

Think of it this way:

Just like water can be ice, liquid, or steam depending on temperature, iron can be different "phases" depending on temperature AND carbon content.

This diagram tells us:

  • What structure the iron has
  • How hard or soft it will be
  • Whether it's steel or cast iron

The Phase Diagram

Understanding the Axes

Horizontal Axis (X)

Carbon Percentage (0 to 6.67%)

• More carbon = Harder material

• Less carbon = Softer material

Vertical Axis (Y)

Temperature (°C)

• Higher temp = Different structure

• As you cool down, iron changes

The Main Phases (What Iron Becomes)

1Ferrite (α-iron)

Atoms/cell: 2

Packing: 68%

Carbon: Max 0.022%

Temp: Below 912°C

Properties: Soft, bendable, magnetic

Example: Soft iron, mild steel

2Austenite (γ-iron)

Atoms/cell: 4

Packing: 74%

Carbon: Up to 2.14%

Temp: 912-1394°C

Properties: Can hold more carbon, not magnetic

Important: This is where heat treatment starts!

3Cementite (Fe₃C)

Formula: Fe₃C

Carbon: Exactly 6.67%

Hardness: ~800 HV

Nature: Metastable

Properties: Very hard, very brittle

Note: Too brittle to use alone - needs ferrite

4Pearlite

Mix: 88% Ferrite + 12% Fe₃C

Forms at: 727°C

Hardness: ~200 HV

Strength: ~400 MPa

What it is: Mix of Ferrite + Cementite in layers

Properties: Good balance of strength and hardness

The Most Important Point: Eutectoid

The Magic Spot: 0.76% Carbon at 727°C

This is THE most important point in steel making!

What happens here?

Austenite → Ferrite + Cementite (Pearlite) When you cool through 727°C, austenite transforms into pearlite

Why is this important?

  • It divides steels into three types
  • Most heat treatments aim for this temperature
  • The product (pearlite) has great properties
Steel Type Carbon Content What You Get
Hypoeutectoid Less than 0.76% C Ferrite + Pearlite (softer)
Eutectoid Exactly 0.76% C 100% Pearlite (balanced)
Hypereutectoid More than 0.76% C Cementite + Pearlite (harder)

Steel vs Cast Iron (The Big Divide)

The vertical line at 2.14% Carbon splits the diagram into two worlds:

STEEL (Left Side)

Carbon: 0 to 2.14%

Can you bend it? Yes

Can you weld it? Yes

Can you shape it? Yes (rolling, forging)

Uses: Buildings, cars, tools, machines

CAST IRON (Right Side)

Carbon: 2.14 to 6.67%

Can you bend it? No (breaks)

Can you weld it? Difficult

Can you shape it? Only by casting (melting & pouring)

Uses: Engine blocks, pipes, cookware

Simple rule: Want to shape it after making? → Use steel (low carbon). Want to pour it into a mold? → Use cast iron (high carbon).

How to Use This Diagram

Example: What happens when you cool 0.4% carbon steel?

Step 1: At 1000°C → You're in the Austenite region (100% austenite)

Step 2: Cool down to 800°C → Some ferrite starts forming

Step 3: Hit 727°C → The remaining austenite becomes pearlite

Final result: You get a mix of Ferrite (soft) + Pearlite (harder)

This tells you:

  • The final structure
  • How hard it will be
  • How strong it will be
  • How to heat treat it

Why This Diagram Matters

1. Choose the Right Material

Need something soft and bendable? Use low carbon (0.1-0.2%).

Need something hard? Use high carbon (0.8-1%).

2. Design Heat Treatment

The diagram tells you what temperatures to use for hardening, softening, or strengthening steel.

3. Predict Properties

Know the carbon % and cooling process? The diagram predicts hardness and strength!

4. Understand Failures

If steel broke or cracked, this diagram helps explain why (wrong structure formed).

Key Points to Remember

  • The diagram shows what iron looks like at different temperatures and carbon levels
  • The eutectoid point (0.76% C, 727°C) is the MOST important spot
  • 2.14% carbon divides steel from cast iron
  • More carbon = harder but more brittle
  • Use this diagram to choose materials and design heat treatments
