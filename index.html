1. Introduction to Phase Diagrams

The Iron-Carbon (Fe-C) phase diagram, also known as the Fe-Fe‚ÇÉC diagram, is a fundamental tool in materials science and metallurgical engineering. It represents the equilibrium phases present in Fe-C alloys at different temperatures and compositions under slow cooling conditions.

This diagram is limited to carbon concentrations up to 6.67 wt% C (the composition of cementite, Fe‚ÇÉC), as this represents the practical upper limit for commercial Fe-C alloys.

This is an equilibrium diagram, meaning it assumes infinitely slow cooling rates that allow the system to reach thermodynamic equilibrium at each temperature. In practice, cooling rates affect the final microstructure, leading to non-equilibrium phases (e.g., martensite in quenched steels).

5. Microstructural Constituents

Pearlite (Eutectoid Microstructure)

Pearlite is not a phase but a lamellar microstructure consisting of alternating layers of Œ±-ferrite and cementite (Fe‚ÇÉC). It forms when austenite transforms at the eutectoid temperature.

Œ≥ (0.76% C) ‚Üí Œ± (0.022% C) + Fe‚ÇÉC (6.67% C)
Austenite ‚Üí Pearlite (ferrite + cementite)

Pearlite Formation Mechanism

During slow cooling through 727¬∞C, the austenite (Œ≥) decomposes simultaneously into:

  • Carbon-depleted ferrite (Œ±) - forms the light layers
  • Carbon-enriched cementite (Fe‚ÇÉC) - forms the dark layers

The carbon rejected from forming ferrite diffuses to adjacent regions where cementite nucleates, creating the characteristic layered structure.

Ledeburite (Eutectic Microstructure)

Ledeburite forms during the eutectic reaction at 1147¬∞C in cast irons. It consists of austenite and cementite that solidify simultaneously from the liquid.

Liquid (4.3% C) ‚Üí Œ≥ (2.14% C) + Fe‚ÇÉC (6.67% C)

Upon further cooling below 727¬∞C, the austenite in ledeburite transforms to pearlite, resulting in a final structure called transformed ledeburite (pearlite + cementite).

6. Invariant Reactions

The Fe-C system contains three important invariant reactions where three phases coexist in equilibrium at a fixed temperature and composition.

6.1 Eutectoid Reaction MOST CRITICAL

‚ö° Eutectoid Transformation

Temperature: 727¬∞C (1341¬∞F)

Composition: 0.76 wt% C (also called 0.8% C in many texts)

Reaction:

Œ≥ (Austenite) ‚ü∂ Œ± (Ferrite) + Fe‚ÇÉC (Cementite)

This is the single most important reaction in steel metallurgy because:

  • It defines the boundary between hypoeutectoid (< 0.76% C) and hypereutectoid (> 0.76% C) steels
  • The product (pearlite) has excellent mechanical properties
  • Most heat treatment processes involve heating above or cooling through this temperature
  • The A‚ÇÅ temperature (727¬∞C) is a critical reference in heat treatment
Steel Classification:
‚Ä¢ Hypoeutectoid steels (< 0.76% C): Form proeutectoid ferrite + pearlite
‚Ä¢ Eutectoid steel (0.76% C): Forms 100% pearlite
‚Ä¢ Hypereutectoid steels (> 0.76% C): Form proeutectoid cementite + pearlite

6.2 Eutectic Reaction

üå°Ô∏è Eutectic Transformation

Reaction:

This reaction is important for cast irons:

  • Determines the solidification behavior of high-carbon alloys
  • Cast irons with ~4.3% C have the lowest melting point, improving castability

The boundary at 2.14 wt% C (the maximum solubility of carbon in austenite) fundamentally divides ferrous alloys into two categories:

  • Low-carbon (Mild) steel: 0.05-0.25% C - Ductile, weldable, used in construction
  • Medium-carbon steel: 0.25-0.6% C - Balanced strength/ductility, gears, shafts
  • High-carbon steel: 0.6-1.0% C - Hard, strong, springs, cutting tools
  • Ultra-high carbon steel: 1.0-2.0% C - Very hard, specialized cutting tools

8. Practical Applications of the Fe-C Diagram

8.1 Heat Treatment Design

The phase diagram is essential for designing heat treatment processes:

Annealing

Heat above A‚ÇÉ line (austenite region), hold, then slowly cool to produce soft, ductile microstructure (ferrite + pearlite).

Purpose: Reduce hardness, improve machinability

Normalizing

Heat above A‚ÇÉ, hold, then air cool. Produces finer pearlite than annealing.

Purpose: Refine grain structure, improve uniformity

Hardening

Heat above A‚ÇÉ to form austenite, then rapidly quench to form martensite (non-equilibrium).

Purpose: Maximum hardness and strength

Spheroidizing

Heat to just below A‚ÇÅ (727¬∞C) and hold for extended time. Cementite forms spheroids instead of lamellae.

Purpose: Improve machinability of high-carbon steels

8.2 Microstructure Prediction

Engineers use the diagram to predict the final microstructure:

Example: 0.4% C steel cooled slowly from 1000¬∞C

1000¬∞C: 100% Austenite (Œ≥)
‚Üì Cool through A‚ÇÉ line (~800¬∞C)
800-727¬∞C: Proeutectoid Œ±-ferrite begins forming
727¬∞C: Remaining austenite ‚Üí Pearlite
Room temp: ~50% Ferrite + 50% Pearlite

8.3 Alloy Composition Selection

Material selection based on required properties:

  • High ductility needed? ‚Üí Choose low-carbon steel (< 0.2% C)
  • Balance of strength and ductility? ‚Üí Medium-carbon steel (0.3-0.5% C)
  • Maximum hardness? ‚Üí High-carbon or tool steel (0.8-1.2% C)
  • Complex casting shapes? ‚Üí Cast iron near eutectic (~4.3% C)

9. Limitations and Considerations

‚ö†Ô∏è Critical Limitations of the Equilibrium Diagram

1. Assumes Equilibrium Conditions

The diagram assumes infinitely slow cooling that allows complete diffusion and equilibrium. In reality:

  • Industrial cooling rates prevent equilibrium
  • Faster cooling ‚Üí finer pearlite, bainite, or martensite (not shown on diagram)
  • TTT and CCT diagrams are needed for non-equilibrium transformations

2. Fe-Fe‚ÇÉC System (Metastable)

The diagram shows the metastable Fe-Fe‚ÇÉC system, not the stable Fe-Graphite system:

  • Cementite (Fe‚ÇÉC) can decompose to Fe + Graphite given enough time/temperature
  • In gray cast iron, graphite forms instead of cementite
  • Silicon promotes graphitization

3. Pure Binary System

Real steels contain alloying elements (Mn, Si, Cr, Ni, Mo) that:

  • Shift phase boundaries (A‚ÇÅ, A‚ÇÉ temperatures)
  • Stabilize or destabilize certain phases
  • Change transformation kinetics
  • Require multi-component phase diagrams for accuracy

4. No Information on Kinetics

The diagram shows what forms, not how fast:

  • Transformation rates depend on temperature and composition
  • Nucleation and growth mechanisms are not indicated
  • Diffusion distances and times are not considered

üîß Complementary Tools Required

For complete understanding of steel behavior, engineers also use:

  • TTT Diagrams (Time-Temperature-Transformation): Show isothermal transformation kinetics
  • CCT Diagrams (Continuous Cooling Transformation): Show transformations during continuous cooling
  • Pseudo-binary diagrams: For specific alloy systems (e.g., Fe-C-Mn)
  • Schaeffler diagrams: For predicting weld microstructures in stainless steels
